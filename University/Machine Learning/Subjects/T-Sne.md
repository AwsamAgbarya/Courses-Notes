What about Non-Euclidean Pairwise
Distances? ^hsQSlZk3

More often than not we also want to find relations and manifolds of data that is non-euclidean
The reason this data is non-euclidean is because it doesnt follow euclidean rules and cannot be represented
as vectors, but instead has to stay as metadata. ^SvmVIfgO

Given the dissimilarity matrix D such that dij is the euclidean distance between xi and xj
dij has to adhere to the following rules for it to be measuring metric data:

1) dij >=0, distance cant be negative

2) dij=0 only if i and j are the same

3) dij=dji distance between two points is the same
no matter where u look at it from

4) dij <= dik + dkj Triangle inequality ^nAoqguq7

How does non-metric data violate metric rules?


1) non-metric similarities dont have to abide by the triangle inequality
if we have one black chair, one white chair and one black door, the black door
and char are similar in color and thus should be somewhat close to each other, the
two chairs are also similar in shape and should be close to each other, Euclidean space
tells us that the white chair is by proxy also close to the black door which in this case is not
true! This is called Intransitive similarities


2) A point can only be a center of a finite amount of point in metric data
Lets say we have point A as the center of B C and D, and its also the center of
E, G and F. In Euclidean space we cannot model A is the nearest neighbor for all those points
according to the hexagon distance to center theorem, when in reality we can have an infinite amount
of points that are closest neighbors to A 


3) Asymmetries in distances
in non-metric data, the distance from A to be B can be different from the distance from B to A
take for example the distance as a measure of how close of a friend A is to B, A might consider Bas 
a close friend whereas B might not, these asymmetries are not considered in Euclidean space!! ^HPDKBgQy

matrix D is also called squared Euclidean if it follows the l2 norm as a measurement of dissimilarities

Now with non-metric data we want to be able to model dissimilarities using non l2 norm, this can be done
but it imposes a new problem, our D matrix is no longer squared Euclidean
if D is squared euclidean then it is PSD and is called the covariance matrix (after double centering)
if D is not squared Euclidean then it is not PSD and is called the pseudo covariance matrix (no longer convariance
since its not PSD)
Since it is no longer PSD, it can have negative eigenvalues, which according to most of our applications we ignore
but it turns out that negative eigenvectors are also a measure of similairity that we should consider in non-metric data!
